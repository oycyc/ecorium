Transportation

The transportation sector generates 27% of all GHG emissions in 2020, primarily from burning fossil fuels to power for trucks, cars, ships, trains, and planes.[1]

But how has the transportation sector kept up with the increased use of renewable energy for electricity?

Car Fuel Types

Gasoline Vehicles

Gasoline vehicles use a internal combustion engine. When the fuel and air combine in the combustion chamber, they ignite a spark from the spark plug and convert the gasoline into motion so that the car can move.[x]

  • ✓ 8,887 grams of tailpipe CO2 created/gallon of gasoline[x]
  • ✓ emits methane and nitrous oxide (higher global warming potential gases) from the tailpipe[x]
  • ✓ fast[x]

Fully Electric Vehicles

Fully electric vehicles uses a traction battery to power the electric motor and must be plugged in to a wall outlet or charging equipment.[x]

  • ✓ emits no exhaust from the tailpipe[x]
  • over 47,000 stations available nationally as of April 29, 2022[x]
  • ✓ emissions are created during the production and distribution of the electricity[x]

Hybrid Electric Vehicles

Hybrid electric vehicles have an internal combustion engine and an electric motor that uses energy stored in batteries. Instead of charging by plugging into sources of electricity, the batteries are recharged through regenerative braking.[x]

  • ✓ produces lower emissions compared with gas-only vehicles[x]
  • ✓ absdf[x]
  • ✓ fast[x]

Even though EVs have no tailpipe emissions, emissions are created during the production and distribution of the electricity used to fuel the vehicle.[2]

Regardless, there are good news:

In 2021, 20% of electricity produced was made from renewable sources, signaling a positive shift.[3]
